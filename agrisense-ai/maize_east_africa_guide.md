# East Africa Maize Production Guide
## Smallholder Farmer Edition
### Covers: Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Ethiopia

---

## 1. Introduction

Maize (Zea mays) is the most important food and cash crop for smallholder farmers in East Africa. It is the primary staple for over 300 million people in the region. Smallholder farmers growing 0.5 to 5 hectares account for over 75% of total maize production.

This guide covers everything from land preparation to post-harvest storage using locally available inputs and climate-smart practices.

## 2. Agroecological Zones and Planting Calendars

### Kenya

| Zone | Description | Long Rains (Season A) | Short Rains (Season B) |
|------|-------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Zone I — Highlands (1800m+) | Central, Nyeri, Kirinyaga | March–April | October–November |
| Zone II — Upper Midlands (1200–1800m) | Rift Valley, Nakuru, Meru | February–March | October |
| Zone III — Lower Midlands (900–1200m) | Western Kenya, Kisii, Kakamega | March | August–September |
| Zone IV — ASALs | North Eastern, Coast inland | Wait for rains | April–May (long rains), November (short) |

**Rule of thumb for Kenya**: Plant at the onset of the rains — when the soil is wet to at least 10cm depth. Do not plant in anticipation of rain; you risk seed rotting.

## 3. Soil Preparation

### Minimum tillage (recommended)
- Reduces soil erosion, maintains soil moisture, improves organic matter
- Use hand hoe or ripper attachment on ox-plough
- Remove crop residues or incorporate them (improves organic matter)

### Full tillage (for heavy soils or high-weed pressure)
- Plough to 20–25cm depth before rains
- Harrow to break clods
- Mark rows using a rope or furrow plough

### Soil testing
**STRONGLY RECOMMENDED before planting.** Contact your county agricultural office or use a certified private soil lab:
- Kenya: Crop Nutrition Laboratory (KARI Kabete), SGS Kenya
- Tanzania: Sokoine University Agricultural Lab, TPRI
- Uganda: Kawanda Agricultural Research Institute

Soil test measures: pH, N, P, K, organic matter, micronutrients. Cost: KES 1,500–3,500 for full panel.

## 4. Seed Selection

### Selection criteria
1. **Maturity period**: Match variety maturity to your rainy season length
2. **Disease tolerance**: Choose MLN-tolerant varieties in East Africa
3. **Drought tolerance**: For areas with erratic rainfall (ASALs, dry years)
4. **Market preference**: Does your local buyer prefer white or yellow maize?

### Recommended varieties by zone

**Highlands (above 1,500m) — Kenya**
- H614D (120 days, 7–9 t/ha) — reliable, popular
- DK8031 (115 days, 8–10 t/ha) — top yielder, needs good management
- SEEDCO SC403 (110 days, 7–8 t/ha) — MLN tolerant

**Mid-altitude (900–1,500m)**
- H6213 (110 days, 7–9 t/ha)
- PHB 3253 (112 days, 8 t/ha)
- PANNAR PAN 691 (108 days, 7–8 t/ha)

**Drought-prone and ASALs**
- WEMA DTMA varieties (90 days, 5–6 t/ha) — specifically bred for drought
- SEEDCO SC301 (90 days, 4–5 t/ha) — very early maturing for short rains

**Note**: Always buy certified seed in ORIGINAL SEALED packets from a KEPHIS-accredited dealer. Fake seed is a significant problem. Check the KEPHIS certification number on the packet.

## 5. Planting

### Spacing
**Optimal for East Africa smallholder conditions**:
- Row spacing: 75cm between rows
- Plant spacing: 25cm between plants within row
- One seed per hole (certified hybrid seed — not two as commonly practiced with OPVs)
- Plant population: 53,000 plants per hectare = 21,400 plants per acre

### Planting depth
- 3–5cm deep (no more — deep planting delays emergence)
- Soil must be moist at planting depth

### Planting method
1. Make holes with a stick or jab planter
2. Drop ONE seed per hole
3. Add 3–5g (one teaspoon) of DAP fertiliser beside the seed — NOT touching the seed
4. Cover with loose soil — do not compact

## 6. Fertiliser Programme

### THE GOLDEN RULE: Always fertilise when soil is moist
Never apply fertiliser on dry soil — it causes root burn and volatilisation loss.

### Basal fertiliser (at planting)

| Product | Rate per acre | Rate per hectare | Application |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| DAP (18-46-0) | 50 kg (1 bag) | 125 kg | In hole beside seed |
| NPK 17-17-17 | 50 kg | 125 kg | In hole beside seed |
| Compost/manure | 2 tonnes | 5 tonnes | Incorporated pre-planting |

**Calculating for your field size:**
- 0.5 acre: Half a bag of DAP (25 kg)
- 1 acre: 1 bag of DAP (50 kg)
- 2 acres: 2 bags of DAP (100 kg)
- 1 hectare: 2.5 bags of DAP (125 kg)

### Top dressing (4–6 weeks after planting, V4–V6 stage)

| Product | Rate per acre | Rate per hectare | Timing |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|--------|
| CAN (26% N) | 50 kg (1 bag) | 125 kg | V4–V6 stage |
| Urea (46% N) | 28 kg (0.56 bag) | 70 kg | V4–V6 stage (use with caution) |

**Identify V4–V6**: Count 4–6 leaves fully collared. Plant is typically knee-high to thigh-high.

### Integrated Soil Fertility Management (ISFM) — recommended
Use HALF the recommended inorganic fertiliser + organic matter for best results on degraded soils:
- 25 kg DAP + 1 tonne compost at planting
- 25 kg CAN at top-dressing
- This improves soil health over time while maintaining reasonable yields

### Micronutrient deficiencies common in East Africa

| Nutrient | Deficiency symptom | Correction |
|----------|-------------------|------------|
| Zinc (Zn) | White striping on young leaves | Apply 5 kg ZnSO₄ per acre at planting |
| Boron (B) | Barren cobs, poor kernel set | Apply 0.5 kg Borax per acre |
| Sulfur (S) | General yellowing of young leaves | DAP contains sulfur; or use NPKS fertilisers |

## 7. Weed Management

### Critical period
Maize must be weed-free for the first 4–6 weeks after planting. Weeds during this period can reduce yield by 50–80%.

### Approach
1. **First weeding**: 2–3 weeks after planting (when weeds are small)
2. **Second weeding**: At top-dressing time (4–6 weeks)
3. **Third weeding**: If weed pressure is high, 8 weeks after planting

### Herbicides (minimum tillage approach)
- Pre-emergence: Atrazine + S-metolachlor (e.g. Primagram Gold) — apply immediately after planting before weeds emerge
- Post-emergence: Nicosulfuron (e.g. Accent) for grass weeds in maize — apply at V3–V5 stage

**Caution**: Always read herbicide labels carefully. Atrazine cannot be used near water sources.

## 8. Pest and Disease Management

### Key pests
See disease knowledge base for full details. Key threats to monitor:
1. **Fall Armyworm** (Spodoptera frugiperda) — scout from V1 stage; spray into whorl
2. **Maize Stalk Borer** (Busseola fusca) — white larvae in whorl; Bt or Chlorpyrifos
3. **Aphids** — control to reduce virus transmission; use imidacloprid if severe

### Key diseases
1. **Maize Lethal Necrosis (MLN)** — no cure; rogue infected plants; notify extension officer
2. **Gray Leaf Spot** — use tolerant varieties; fungicide if severe
3. **Common Rust** — tolerant varieties; mancozeb if needed

### Integrated Pest Management (IPM)
1. Scout every 7 days from emergence
2. Use economic thresholds before spraying
3. Prefer biological and mechanical control where effective
4. Use PPE (gloves, mask, goggles) when applying pesticides
5. Follow label re-entry intervals before returning to field

## 9. Harvesting

### When to harvest
- Physiological maturity (dry matter maximum): when husk turns brown, black layer visible at grain base
- Grain moisture at physiological maturity: ~30–35%
- Optimal storage moisture: ≤13.5%

### Drying
- Dry on clean tarpaulins in sun — avoid drying on bare soil (contamination risk)
- Turn regularly for even drying
- Target: grain snaps cleanly when bitten = dry enough

### Signs grain is too wet to store
- Grain dents when pressed with fingernail
- Grain feels cool and heavy
- Grain sticks together
- Mold smell

## 10. Post-harvest Storage

### Hermetic storage (RECOMMENDED)
- SuperGrain bags, PICS bags, or metal silos
- Eliminates weevils and aflatoxin risk without chemicals
- Cost: KES 700–1,200 for a 100kg SuperGrain bag
- Grain lasts 6–12 months with no chemical treatment

### Chemical treatment
- If not using hermetic: Actellic Super dust at 50g per 90kg bag
- Apply in layers as you fill the bag
- Wear gloves and mask during application
- Do not eat grain within 3 months of treatment

### Aflatoxin prevention
Aflatoxin (from Aspergillus flavus mold) is a severe health and market risk. Prevention:
- Harvest at correct moisture (don't leave in field too long after maturity)
- Dry quickly to below 13.5% moisture
- Use hermetic storage
- Sort and discard any discolored, shriveled, or mold-infected grains before storage
- Test aflatoxin level if selling to formal markets (required above 10 ppb limit in Kenya)

| Management level | Yield range | Notes |
|-----------------|------------|-------|
| Subsistence (no inputs) | 0.5–1.0 t/ha | National average in many years |

| Recommended practices | 4.0–7.0 t/ha | With full fertiliser + good seed + pest control |
